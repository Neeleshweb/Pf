About
From a Parking Ticket to Product

My path into product didn't start in a boardroom — it started behind the wheel.

While completing my master's in Halifax, I worked part-time as a food-delivery driver. Parking was a constant, expensive battle. Instead of just complaining, I did what a PM does: I validated the problem with data. I built a survey, shared it widely, and found that 96% of 34 respondents felt the same pain. Identifying a real problem, proving it with evidence, and imagining a solution — that's the moment product management chose me.

Since then I've channeled that instinct into products that solve real problems with measurable results. At CoreLogic, I led AI and integration initiatives that grew a real-estate platform past 1 million users. Before product, I spent years as a software engineer — which means I can sit with engineers in the architecture and with executives in the strategy, and translate seamlessly between them.

Outside of work, I write books on product management and experiment in the kitchen — both reflect the same belief: that the best results come from curiosity, iteration, and a little bit of bold.

Featured Case Study
Anatomy of a 0 → 1 Build
CoreLogic · Centralized Service Layer

One Source of Truth Across Two Products

The Problem

MLSTOUCH and OneHome ran on disconnected data. A listing favorited on one app wouldn't appear on the other; some listings showed up on one platform and not the other. The result: confused users, 1-star reviews, and a flood of support tickets.

My Approach

Rather than patch each symptom, I diagnosed the root cause — no shared data layer. I aligned engineering, UX, and QA around building a Centralized Service Layer from scratch, defined the requirements bridging business needs and technical constraints, and sequenced delivery to de-risk the migration.

The Outcome

A single synchronized source of truth across both products. Data inconsistencies disappeared, the experience became seamless, and the metrics that matter to the business moved sharply in the right direction.

How I Work
My Product Philosophy
01

Validate before you build

Opinions are cheap; evidence is not. I pressure-test the problem with real users and data before committing a single sprint — the parking survey was just the beginning.

02

Speak both languages

My engineering background means I translate fluently between business goals and technical reality — fewer misunderstandings, faster delivery, more trust on both sides.

03

Tie everything to a metric

If I can't name the number a feature should move, it isn't ready. Every initiative I lead has a measurable outcome attached from day one.

04

Fix root causes, not symptoms

It's tempting to patch the visible problem. I dig for the underlying cause — which is how a sync bug became a centralized platform that lifted retention.
